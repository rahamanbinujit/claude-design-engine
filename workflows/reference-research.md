# Reference Research Workflow

**40% of your design time should be research.** Never design from a blank canvas. Never design from imagination alone. Study what works, then build on it.

---

## Why Research First?

Amateur designers start with a blank HTML file and try to "create."
Professional designers start by studying 10 references and then combining the best patterns.

The difference in output quality is night and day.

---

## The Process

### Step 1: Define What You're Looking For
Before searching, know:
- What platform is this for?
- What topic/niche?
- What format? (single post, carousel, thumbnail)
- What mood? (bold, minimal, playful, editorial)

### Step 2: Search for References
Look at these sources (in order of usefulness):
1. **The platform itself** — Search the actual platform for top-performing posts in your niche
2. **Behance** — Search for "[platform] [content type] design" (e.g., "LinkedIn carousel design")
3. **Dribbble** — Search for "[style] social media" (e.g., "dark social media post")
4. **Pinterest** — Search for "[niche] infographic" or "[niche] poster design"
5. **Competitor accounts** — What are the top creators in your niche posting?

### Step 3: Analyze (Don't Just Look)

For each reference, extract:

| Question | Note |
|---|---|
| What's the layout structure? | (grid, centered, asymmetric, split) |
| How many text elements? | (headline only? + subtitle? + body?) |
| What's the font style? | (serif, sans-serif, display, handwritten) |
| What's the color count? | (2 colors? 3? monochrome?) |
| What's the accent treatment? | (color highlight, underline, glow, size) |
| What creates visual hierarchy? | (size contrast, weight, color, position) |
| Why would someone stop scrolling? | (bold text, face, contrast, curiosity gap) |

### Step 4: Extract Patterns

After analyzing 3-5 references, identify the common patterns:
- "All top posts in this niche use dark backgrounds"
- "The best ones have max 5 words on the cover"
- "Face photos + minimal text outperform designed graphics"
- "Stats presented large with one highlighted in accent color"

### Step 5: Apply to Your Design

Use the patterns as constraints, not templates:
- Match the structural pattern that works (layout, text count)
- Apply your own brand (colors, fonts, identity from config)
- Add your own creative interpretation
- The goal is to be **informed by** references, not to **copy** them

---

## What NOT to Do

- Don't skip research because "you already know what looks good"
- Don't copy a single reference exactly
- Don't search for 30 minutes — 5-10 minutes is enough
- Don't use references from a completely different platform (Instagram trends != LinkedIn trends)
- Don't ignore patterns that contradict your assumptions

---

## Quick Research Templates

### For LinkedIn posts:
Search: "[topic] linkedin post design", "[topic] linkedin carousel"
Look at: Top 5 creators posting about this topic. What visuals do they use?

### For YouTube thumbnails:
Search the topic on YouTube. Sort by view count. Study the top 10 thumbnails.
What faces? What text? What colors? What's the visual pattern?

### For Instagram:
Search: "[niche] instagram post design", "[niche] infographic"
Check: What's trending in your niche's explore page?

---

## The 40% Rule

If your total design time is 30 minutes:
- 12 minutes: Research and reference analysis
- 8 minutes: Building the HTML/CSS
- 5 minutes: Self-review and iteration
- 5 minutes: Export and final check

Research is not wasted time. It's the difference between "nice design" and "stop-scrolling design."
